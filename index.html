What is mPOS?

The Core Idea Globally, when a customer transfers money between two banks — even domestically — the banks do not physically move money in real time. They update internal ledgers and settle the difference later. This is how banking has always worked.

mPOS proposes the same principle for international transfers. Instead of routing money physically across borders through multiple intermediaries, mPOS coordinates local payouts on each side and balances the obligation digitally through an international accounting ledger.

We are not doing anything outside the box. We are proposing that banks extend their existing domestic ledger logic globally — and creating the infrastructure, market, and rules to make it work securely, efficiently, and at scale. Why this matters: Banks already understand ledger-based settlement. mPOS speaks their language — and gives them infrastructure they can embed without rebuilding anything.
💡 The customer sees their bank. The bank uses mPOS underneath — just like Visa and Mastercard power card payments invisibly inside banking platforms.

👥 Agents — The Distributed Liquidity Layer
Agents are the local liquidity providers that make cross-border settlement possible without prefunding. They are ordinary bank customers — not employees, not external companies — who earn a fee for participating.

Who Are Agents?

Distributed Liquidity Without Prefunding Traditional cross-border rails require banks to prefund accounts in each destination country — tying up capital that sits idle until needed. This is expensive and creates the very imbalances that cause FX backlogs.

mPOS uses a different model. Agents are accredited bank customers with available liquidity who consent to participate in the network. They provide local fiat on demand — and are compensated with a share of the transaction fee. The network never needs a central reserve pool. Why this works: Agents exist in every corridor naturally. Their participation creates a self-sustaining distributed liquidity layer that scales with transaction volume.
⚠️ Agents are randomly assigned by the engine. The sender never knows who their agent is. The agent never knows who the sender is. This prevents coordination, manipulation, and money laundering rings.

Nigeria Agents 🇳🇬 — Sender Side

💡 For the sender side, the engine checks the agent's international ledger balance — not their savings account. The ledger balance is what facilitates the transaction. It is denominated in their own local currency (₦) and is not a separate currency or token.

UK Agents 🇬🇧 — Recipient Side

💡 For the recipient side, the engine checks the agent's GBP fiat liquidity — because they need actual GBP to pay the recipient locally inside the UK banking system.
🔄 Conversion — Keeping the Ledger Circulating
After facilitating a transaction, an agent holds an international ledger balance. They must convert this back to local fiat — by exchanging with another agent in the same country. This cycle keeps the network healthy and prevents corridor stagnation.

Why Conversion Matters

The 3-Day Rule — Anti-Stagnation Mechanism If agents held ledger balances indefinitely, corridors would stall — liquidity would accumulate on one side and dry up on the other. The 3-day conversion rule ensures that ledger value stays moving.

Agents can convert immediately — there is no waiting requirement. The 3 days is a maximum window. Agents who convert promptly pay a lower fee. Agents who delay are automatically converted by the engine at a higher fee.

Exchange is only permitted between agents in the same country. Cross-border ledger exchange is blocked — this prevents bad actors from accumulating ledger balances across borders to manufacture shortages. Why this matters: The conversion cycle is what makes the network self-sustaining. It is the heartbeat of the system.

Collective Network-Wide Stabilisation 0.2% of every transaction is retained on the recipient side in the destination currency. This accumulates collectively across all corridors flowing into a destination.

What the Ledger Actually Is

Extending Domestic Logic to Global Scale When you transfer money between two domestic banks today, no physical cash moves. The banks update their internal ledgers and settle the net difference later. This is standard banking practice — globally understood and trusted.

mPOS proposes extending this exact logic internationally. Banks already collaborate on domestic ledger settlement. We are proposing a framework for global banks to collaborate on an international ledger — with the market structure, rules, and infrastructure to make it work securely and at scale.

The ledger balance appears in agents' bank apps as a standard account line — same currency symbol, same denomination. It is simply labelled "International" alongside their normal savings or current account. Why regulators will accept this: It is not a new currency. It is not shadow banking. It is a formalised extension of ledger-based settlement that banks already practice domestically.

⚖️ How Existing Rails Work — And Why They Struggle
mPOS is not trying to compete with SWIFT or correspondent banking. It is modernising the underlying logic. Understanding how existing rails work — and where they fail — explains exactly why mPOS exists.

How SWIFT & Correspondent Banking Works

The Current System — Step by Step When a bank in Nigeria wants to send money to a bank in the UK, there is rarely a direct relationship between them. Instead, the transaction passes through a chain of correspondent banks — each one holding an account at the next.

A typical Nigeria → UK transfer might pass through: Nigerian bank → correspondent bank in Lagos → correspondent bank in London → recipient UK bank. Each hop adds time, cost, and a deduction.

The key problem is the settlement model. Each bank along the chain uses prefunded nostro/vostro accounts — meaning money must sit idle in destination accounts before transfers can happen. These prefunded pools must be constantly balanced. Why this creates imbalance: If Nigeria consistently sends more to the UK than the UK sends back to Nigeria, the UK-side pools fill up and the Nigeria-side pools drain. Banks must rebalance — which means higher fees, slower processing, and eventually FX backlogs that force central bank intervention.

The Imbalance Problem — Why CBN Hates It

How FX Backlogs Form Existing rails use an "after-settlement" model. They route transactions but depend on periodic gross settlement — calculating how much Nigerian banks have facilitated to UK banks versus how much UK banks have facilitated back — and then netting the difference.

When Nigeria sends significantly more outbound than it receives inbound over time, the imbalance accumulates. Banks respond by increasing fees to discourage outbound. Backlogs form in the FX settlement queue. The Central Bank of Nigeria is forced to intervene — releasing reserves, adjusting policies, and sometimes restricting transactions — to clear the backlog.

Customers and businesses become the victims. Fees rise. Transfer limits tighten. Contracts are missed because payments don't arrive on time. Why this matters: The CBN's regulatory sandbox exists precisely because they want better infrastructure. mPOS addresses the imbalance structurally — not with patches.

Why mPOS Is Not a Competitor

Modernising — Not Disrupting SWIFT and correspondent banking are not broken concepts — they are the right idea implemented with 1970s technology and settlement logic. mPOS is not trying to replace them. It is proposing the same principle — banks collaborating on ledger-based settlement — but with modern infrastructure, real-time execution, and structural imbalance correction built in.

The question sceptics ask: "Why don't banks just build their own international ledger?" They can — and some are trying. But the challenge is not the technology. It is the market structure, the rules, the agent network, the imbalance correction mechanism, and the regulatory framework. That is what mPOS provides. Think of it like card payments. Banks could theoretically build their own card networks. But Visa and Mastercard solved the coordination problem — and banks adopted them. mPOS solves the coordination problem for cross-border settlement.

A Note on AML and Security

Every Rail Has This Challenge A common concern: bad actors could attempt to use any payment system — including mPOS — to launder money. This is true of every international payment system in existence, including SWIFT, Wise, and bank wires.

mPOS addresses this at multiple layers: AML screening at the engine level, random agent assignment (preventing coordinated routing), tier limits (controlling transaction size), bank-level KYC (the bank verifies the customer before the API call), and the upcoming Digital Passport (DP) — a continuous identity verification system that goes beyond static KYC to verify living, active identity in real time.

Ultimately, money laundering is a government policy challenge — not a platform challenge. Platforms enforce what regulators mandate. mPOS is designed to exceed current regulatory requirements, not just meet them. The Digital Passport (DP) layer — a PLeNat Technologies product — will provide continuous biometric identity verification for all participants in the mPOS network, adding a layer of security that no existing rail currently offers.
